- Rounded square format (Apple applies the rounding automatically)

## APP STORE RANKING FACTORS — HOW THE ALGORITHM WORKS

The App Store ranks apps based on:

**1. Keyword relevance (biggest lever)**
Your app name, subtitle, and keyword field are all indexed. That's why:
- "Healthy Recipes" is in your app name (highest weight)
- "Meal Planner" is in your subtitle (medium weight)
- "meal prep, low carb, vegan, high protein, keto, cooking timer, grocery" are in your keyword field

**2. Conversion rate**
When someone sees your app in search results, do they tap it? A great icon, strong first screenshot, and good rating improve this. High conversion = App Store pushes you higher.

**3. Ratings & reviews**
The algorithm heavily weights ratings. After someone finishes cooking a recipe, show a rating prompt ("Enjoying Healthy Recipes? Rate us ⭐"). Use SKStoreReviewAPI in Swift — it shows the native iOS prompt. Timing: after a user completes 3 recipes.

**4. Downloads velocity**
A spike in downloads in a short window pushes you up the charts. For launch:
- Post on TikTok/Instagram Reels showing the cooking mode
- Post on Reddit: r/MealPrepSunday, r/HealthyFood, r/EatCheapAndHealthy, r/Fitness
- Submit to Product Hunt on a Tuesday (highest traffic day)
- Email any food/health micro-influencers for a free feature

**5. Retention & engagement**
Apple tracks whether people open your app more than once. The cooking mode and meal prep angle drive re-engagement naturally — people come back on cooking days.

**6. In-app purchases (not applicable yet)**
Apps with IAP get additional algorithmic surface. Something to consider later (e.g., premium recipe packs).

## WRAPPING YOUR WEB APP IN A NATIVE IOS APP

Since your app is currently a web app (HTML/JS), the fastest path to the App Store is:

**Option A — WKWebView wrapper (easiest, ~2 hours)**
Create a minimal Swift/Xcode project that loads your `index.html` in a WKWebView full-screen. This is a legitimate and common approach. Apps like this are approved daily. Add the `SKStoreReviewAPI` call natively.

**Option B — Capacitor (recommended if you want more native features)**
Capacitor (by Ionic) wraps web apps in a proper iOS shell with access to native APIs — push notifications, haptic feedback, etc. Install with `npm install @capacitor/core @capacitor/ios`, run `npx cap init`, then `npx cap add ios`. Open in Xcode, archive, submit.

**Option C — React Native rewrite (best long-term, most effort)**
Rewrite in React Native for true native performance. 2–4 weeks of development. Worth it if you plan to build more features over time.

For your first version, **Option B (Capacitor)** is the sweet spot — it's your existing code, gets you native APIs, and Apple approves it.
